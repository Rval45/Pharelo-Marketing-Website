You noticed your mom gripping the kitchen counter this morning — not leaning on it, gripping it — and by afternoon she seemed fine. Now it's 11 PM and you're wondering if that meant something, and whether you should have written it down.

This is what it feels like to track health changes between doctor visits for an aging parent. Not the organized spreadsheet version. The real version, where things happen between appointments and you don't know which details matter. Three weeks later, sitting in the exam room, the doctor asks "Has anything changed?" and your mind goes blank.

You're not failing at this. You're just doing it without a system.

## Why does the doctor only see a fraction of what's happening?

Your parent's doctor sees them for roughly fifteen minutes every few months. You see them every day, every week, or every time you call and listen to how they sound on the phone. **You have access to health information the doctor will never have — unless you bring it.**

The details that matter most aren't dramatic. They're not the fall or the ER visit. They're the slow stuff that changes so gradually you're not sure it's changed at all.

### The value of functional changes

Your dad eating half his dinner instead of finishing it. Your mom taking longer to get out of the chair. Dizziness complaints that started two weeks ago and haven't stopped. A medication refill that came a few days early, which might mean double-dosing.

These observations are called **functional changes**, and they're often more clinically useful than vital signs taken in the office. A blood pressure reading is one snapshot. Three weeks of your notes about when your parent seems dizzy, how long it lasts, and what they were doing — that gives the doctor a picture they can actually work with.

The problem isn't that you don't notice these things. The problem is nothing catches them between the moment you notice and the moment the doctor asks.

## What should you track between doctor visits?

**Write down any change you notice — you don't need to decide if it's medically significant.** Lower the bar completely. One sentence, a date, and what you saw. No medical terminology needed. No interpretation. Just what happened.

Here's what tracking looks like in practice:

- *March 3 — Dad seemed confused about what day it was. Fine by lunch.*
- *March 7 — Didn't finish dinner again. Third time this week.*
- *March 10 — Said his knee was "acting up." Took Tylenol twice today.*
- *March 14 — Good day. Walked to the mailbox and back without stopping.*

Good days matter too. They establish a baseline. When you can tell the doctor "He walked to the mailbox without trouble in early March, but by late March he stopped halfway," that's a timeline doctors can act on.

### Key categories to watch

Focus on the areas that come up most in appointments for aging parents and reveal meaningful patterns over time.

- **Movement and mobility.** How they get up from a chair. Unsteadiness. Holding walls or furniture. Any falls, even ones they brush off.
- **Eating and drinking.** Appetite changes, skipped meals, possible weight loss, coughing while eating.
- **Sleep, energy, and mood.** Sleeping more or less than usual. Daytime drowsiness. Confusion, even brief. Repeating questions. Withdrawal from activities they enjoy.
- **Pain and medication behavior.** Where pain shows up, how often, and what helps. Missed doses, side effects, pill counts that don't add up, or refills arriving off-schedule.

You won't track all of these every day. Notice whichever ones are relevant to your parent's situation. The categories give you a starting point so you're not guessing what matters. If you're also [preparing for an upcoming appointment](/blog/how-to-prepare-for-elderly-parent-doctor-appointment), these notes become your most valuable resource.

## How do you make health tracking stick when you're already stretched thin?

Most caregivers don't track health changes between doctor visits because every system they've tried feels like one more task on a list that's already too long. **The key principle: make it faster to write it down than to decide whether to write it down.**

### Pick one place and keep it simple

Choose a single spot for notes. A notes app on your phone. A small kitchen notebook. A shared document with a sibling. It doesn't matter what — as long as it's always accessible and you never have to think about where to put things.

Set the lowest possible bar. One sentence. No formatting. If you capture three observations in a week, you're building the habit.

### Attach tracking to what you already do

After a phone call with your parent, take fifteen seconds to jot down anything that stood out. After a visit, type one line before you start the car. After dinner, note whether they ate well.

**Don't go back and organize old notes.** That's the trap. You start a system, skip three days, feel behind, try to reconstruct what happened, and quit. Just pick up where you are. The next observation you write down is the one that starts your record.

If you're [managing care across multiple doctors](/blog/managing-parent-care-with-multiple-doctors), keeping one central log is especially important so no detail gets lost between specialists.

## How do you turn scattered notes into something the doctor can use?

**The week before your parent's next appointment, set aside ten minutes to review your notes.** You're looking for three things: patterns, timelines, and your top concerns.

### Spot patterns and build timelines

Did the same complaint come up multiple times? Did something get worse over a specific period? Did a new symptom appear after a medication change? When did it start — and is it getting better, worse, or holding steady?

### Write a short summary for the visit

Distill your notes into five to ten bullet points and hand them to the doctor at the start of the appointment. This isn't overstepping. It's giving them information they can't get any other way. Most doctors are grateful because it makes the fifteen-minute visit far more productive.

Here's what that summary might look like:

- *Appetite has decreased over the past month — leaving half of most meals*
- *Two episodes of morning confusion (March 3 and March 18) — resolved within hours*
- *Knee pain worsening — taking Tylenol almost daily, up from a few times a week*
- *Generally good spirits but tires out earlier in the afternoon*
- *No falls, but she holds furniture more when she walks*

That's a parent's story told by someone paying attention. It gives the doctor a starting point that's miles ahead of silence. For tips on making sure you also [retain what the doctor says back to you](/blog/how-to-remember-what-the-doctor-said), that's a separate but equally important habit.

### When your notes reveal something worrying

Sometimes a pattern will emerge that scares you. The confusion is happening more often. The weight loss is steeper than you realized. The pain isn't going away.

**You don't have to wait for the next scheduled appointment.** Call the doctor's office, describe what you've observed with specific dates and details, and ask whether it warrants an earlier visit. The nurse triaging your call can relay concrete information rather than a vague concern.

Not everything that worries you will turn out to be serious. But the observations that do matter get caught sooner when someone writes them down. If your parent [receives a new diagnosis](/blog/what-to-do-when-parent-gets-new-diagnosis) as a result, having that written timeline will be invaluable for next steps.

## You're the one connecting the dots

The healthcare system wasn't built to track what happens in your parent's living room between appointments. It tracks lab results, imaging, and vitals taken in a clinic. The slow, daily, human details — how they're eating, whether they seem like themselves, the thing they said that worried you on Tuesday — those live with you.

You don't need a medical background to do this well. You just need to trust that what you're noticing matters — because it does.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What health changes should I track for my aging parent between doctor visits?

Focus on functional changes you observe day to day: mobility shifts, appetite or weight changes, sleep and energy patterns, mood or cognitive differences, pain complaints, and medication behavior like missed doses or early refills. You don't need medical knowledge. Just write a date and one sentence about what you noticed, including good days.

### How do I remember what to tell the doctor at my parent's appointment?

Keep a running log of observations in one place — a phone note, notebook, or shared document. The week before the appointment, review your notes and write five to ten bullet points covering patterns, timelines, and your top concerns. Hand the summary to the doctor at the start of the visit so nothing gets forgotten.

### Is it worth tracking health changes if I only see my parent once a week?

Yes. Even weekly observations reveal patterns a doctor can't see in a fifteen-minute visit. Note how your parent looks, moves, and sounds each time you're together or talk by phone. Over several weeks, those single data points connect into a timeline that shows whether something is improving, worsening, or staying the same.

### When should I call the doctor between scheduled visits?

Call whenever your notes reveal a pattern that concerns you — increasing confusion, unexplained weight loss, or worsening pain. Having specific dates and descriptions ready helps the triage nurse assess urgency. You don't need to diagnose anything. Just share what you've observed and ask whether an earlier appointment is warranted.
