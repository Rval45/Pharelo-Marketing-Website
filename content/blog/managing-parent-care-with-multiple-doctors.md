You just left the cardiologist, and she changed your dad's blood pressure medication. But you're not sure if the neurologist who prescribed his other pills last month needs to know. You're sitting in the car with a new discharge sheet, wondering who to call first.

Managing parent care with multiple doctors is one of the most disorienting parts of caregiving. There's no orientation, no handbook, no single person whose job it is to make sure all the pieces fit together. That person, whether you signed up for it or not, is you.

The hardest part isn't any one appointment. It's the space between them -- where information gets lost, medications conflict, and everyone assumes someone else is keeping track.

## Why Do Your Parent's Doctors Never Talk to Each Other?

**Doctors treat in silos because health systems don't share records well, referrals disappear in fax queues, and no one is assigned to coordinate across providers.** The result is that each doctor makes decisions based on a partial view of your parent's health. You're often the only person who was in every room.

### The records problem

Electronic health records were supposed to fix this. They haven't. Different health systems use different platforms. Notes take days or weeks to sync, if they sync at all. Specialists in private practice may not be connected to the hospital system where your parent's primary care doctor works.

### Referrals that vanish

A specialist says "I'm sending a referral to gastroenterology" and you assume it's handled. Three weeks later, nothing. The referral may have been sent, but it landed in a queue, or the fax failed, or the receiving office needs you to call and schedule it yourself. **Always call the receiving office within a week to confirm they have the referral and to get on the calendar.**

### Test results that go nowhere

If a doctor orders bloodwork or imaging, ask two questions before you leave: *"Where will the results go?"* and *"How will we hear about them?"* Don't assume your parent's other doctors will see them. They probably won't.

### Medication lists that drift

Your parent's primary care doctor has one medication list. The cardiologist has another. The pharmacy has a third. After two or three specialist visits with changes, none of these lists match. Medication discrepancies between providers are one of the most common safety issues in elder care. **After every visit that changes a medication, call your parent's primary care office and the pharmacy to update them.**

None of this is your fault. It makes you a person dealing with a system that wasn't designed for coordination. Recognizing that is the starting point for building a better approach.

## How Can You Coordinate Between Your Parent's Doctors Without Burning Out?

**Focus on being the bridge, not the entire infrastructure.** You don't need to understand every medical detail. You need to make sure the right information gets to the right doctor at the right time. A few focused habits will do more than trying to track everything yourself.

### Keep one master document

Create a single page with your parent's current medications (name, dose, frequency, prescribing doctor), active diagnoses, recent test results, and the date and outcome of the last visit with each provider. Update it after every appointment. It takes five minutes and changes the quality of every conversation you have with every doctor. [Here's a full guide to organizing your parent's medical information](/blog/organizing-medical-information-for-elderly-parent).

### Send a pre-visit update

Most practices have a patient portal or a fax number for records. A few days before your parent's visit, send a brief note: *"Since Dad's last visit with you in January, his cardiologist increased his lisinopril to 20mg, he had a chest CT that was clear, and he's been reporting more fatigue in the afternoons."* Three sentences. Now that doctor walks in with context instead of starting from scratch.

### Pick one anchor provider

This is usually your parent's primary care doctor. Make sure they know about every specialist visit, every medication change, every new diagnosis. If you can only update one office, make it this one. A good primary care doctor will catch conflicts and coordinate the bigger picture.

### Ask specialists to talk to each other

When your parent's cardiologist makes a change, ask: *"Can you send a note to Dr. Patel, his neurologist, about this change?"* Have the other provider's fax number ready. This is a small ask that doctors are generally willing to do. They just don't think of it unless prompted.

### Consider a geriatrician

If your parent is over 75 and seeing multiple specialists, a geriatrician -- a doctor who specializes in older adult health -- can serve as the coordinator you've been trying to be. They look at the whole picture: medications, mobility, cognition, quality of life. For complex situations with many moving parts, a geriatrician can be the person who finally connects the dots.

### Keep a running question list

When the neurologist says something that raises a question for the cardiologist, write it down immediately. A running list organized by provider ensures [the right questions get asked in the right room](/blog/questions-to-ask-at-aging-parent-specialist-appointment).

## How Do You Manage Medications Across Multiple Specialists Safely?

**Use one pharmacy, reconcile after every change, and bring an updated paper list to every appointment.** Managing medications across multiple specialists is where real harm can happen. Drug interactions, duplicate therapies, and conflicting dosage adjustments are more common than most families realize.

### Reconcile after every change

After a doctor adds, removes, or adjusts a medication, sit down with the complete list and make sure everything still makes sense together. If you're not sure about an interaction, call your parent's pharmacist. **Pharmacists are the most underused resource in healthcare -- they can check for interactions in seconds and don't require an appointment.**

### Use one pharmacy

If your parent fills prescriptions at different pharmacies, the safety net disappears. A single pharmacy can flag interactions across all prescriptions automatically. If your parent currently uses more than one, consolidating is one of the highest-impact changes you can make.

### Bring the paper list to every visit

Not the list in the system. Not the one from six months ago. The one you updated after the last visit. Hand it to the medical assistant during intake. Say: *"This is his current list as of this week."* It takes ten seconds and it's more reliable than anything in their chart. [Here's how to prepare before you walk in the door](/blog/how-to-prepare-for-elderly-parent-doctor-appointment).

### Ask for a formal medication review

If your parent has three or more active specialists prescribing medications, ask the primary care doctor to lead a comprehensive medication review. Some practices call it a polypharmacy consultation. You may need to request it directly -- it doesn't always happen on its own.

## How Do You Track Health Changes Between Doctor Visits?

**Write one line each time you notice something -- a symptom, a mood shift, a good day or a bad one -- with the date.** The weeks between appointments are where managing parent care with multiple doctors actually breaks down. Your observations are the raw material that helps doctors make better decisions. But if you don't write them down, they'll be gone by the next appointment.

### Build a simple tracking habit

When you notice something, write one line. Include the date. You're not diagnosing anything. You're collecting data that would otherwise evaporate. [Learn more about tracking health changes between visits](/blog/track-health-changes-between-doctor-visits).

### Patterns emerge over time

Those notes reveal things you'd never catch in the moment. The fatigue that always spikes on Wednesdays. The confusion that started around the same time as a new medication. The appetite changes that correlate with pain levels. **When you walk into an appointment with two months of observations, you're giving the doctor something they can't get any other way.**

### The tool matters less than the habit

Pharelo's Signals feature was built around this exact gap -- giving you a quick way to log what you're noticing and organizing those notes before each visit. But a notebook on the kitchen counter works too. What matters is that you're not starting every appointment from zero.

## You're Holding Together Something the System Wasn't Built to Hold

If you're reading this after a long day of coordinating between doctors who don't communicate, chasing referrals that went nowhere, and trying to remember what the specialist said three weeks ago -- you should know that what you're doing is genuinely hard.

Not because you're doing it wrong. Because the system asks families to be the glue without ever acknowledging it.

You don't need to be a medical professional to do this well. You just need a few good habits, a willingness to ask direct questions, and the stubbornness to follow up when things fall through the cracks. You already have all three, or you wouldn't be here.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the best way to keep track of an elderly parent's medications across multiple doctors?

Keep one master medication list that includes every drug's name, dose, frequency, and prescribing doctor. Update it after every appointment and bring a printed copy to each visit. Use a single pharmacy so interactions get flagged automatically. Call your parent's primary care office after any specialist changes a prescription.

### How do I get my parent's doctors to communicate with each other?

Ask each specialist directly to send visit notes to your parent's other providers. Have fax numbers ready. Designate the primary care doctor as the anchor who receives all updates. Before each appointment, send the office a brief summary of what other doctors have done since the last visit.

### Should my aging parent see a geriatrician?

A geriatrician is most helpful when your parent is over 75, sees three or more specialists, and takes multiple medications. Geriatricians are trained to evaluate the full picture -- medications, mobility, cognition, and quality of life together. They can serve as the central coordinator that the healthcare system otherwise lacks.

### What should I do when a referral from one doctor to another gets lost?

Always call the receiving specialist's office within one week of a referral being sent. Confirm they received it and schedule the appointment yourself. Referrals commonly get lost in fax queues or sit in intake backlogs. Following up is not being pushy -- it is the only reliable way to ensure your parent is seen.
